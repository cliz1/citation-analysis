Abstract. We consider the problem of issuing zero-knowledge proofs
obliviously. In this setting, a prover interacts with a veriямБer to pro-
duce a proof, known only to the veriямБer. The resulting proof cannot
be linked back to the interaction that produced it, and can be veriямБed
non-interactively by anyone. This notion generalizes common approaches
to designing blind signatures, which can be seen as the special case of
proving тАЬknowledge of a signing keyтАЭ, and extends the seminal work of
Camenisch and Stadler (тАЩ97).
We propose two provably-secure constructions of oblivious proofs,
and give three applications of our framework. First, we give a publicly
veriямБable version of the classical DiямГe-Hellman based Oblivious PRF.
This yields new constructions of blind signatures and publicly veriямБable
anonymous tokens. Second, we show how to тАЬupgradeтАЭ keyed-veriямБcation
anonymous credentials (Chase et al., CCSтАЩ14) to also be concurrently
secure blind signatures on the same set of attributes. Our upgrade main-
tains the performance and functionality of the credential in the keyed-
veriямБcation setting, we only change issuance. Finally, we provide a varia-
tion of the U-Prove credential system that is provably one-more unforge-
able with concurrent issuance sessions. This constitutes a ямБx for the
attack illustrated by Benhamouda et al. (EUROCRYPTтАЩ21).
Beyond these example applications, as our results are quite general,
we expect they may enable modular design of new primitives with con-
current security, a goal that has historically been challenging to achieve.

Related Works. For more than two decades, it has been folklore in the cryp-
tographic community that ╬г-protocols may be issued obliviously, but this idea
has not been investigated or ever formalized. Yet, it is a natural question to
generalize Schnorr blind signatures in a similar way that Schnorr signatures
were generalized to prove relations involving discrete logarithms. In fact, it was
also not clear how security could be guaranteed for more than polylogarithmic
concurrent queries due to an underlying assumption called ROS [5,38,42] that
naturally emerges when studying concurrent security of interactive ╬г-protocols.
Similar notions have emerged in the past literature. Belenkiy, Camenisch, and
Chase [4] introduced the notion of randomizable zero-knowledge proofs, demand-
ing that a zero-knowledge proof can be interactively re-randomized without
knowing the witness. The notion applies naturally to GrothтАУSahai proofs [27]
and can be used to achieve delegatable anonymous credentials. To the best of
our knowledge, no pairing-free randomizable proofs are known. De Santis and
Yung [18] developed the concept of meta proofs, in which the holder of a proof
can generate a proof that there exists a proof for the veriямБcation statement, how-
ever this is expensive (when it is possible at all), whereas our approach maintains
nearly the same costs as the base ╬г-protocol.
In the seminal work of Camenisch and Stadler [10] on proofs for statements
about discrete logarithms, we see тАЬblind issuance of ╬г-protocolsтАЭ is left as an
open problem. However, proving concurrent soundness for these protocols has
been historically hard due to the so-called ROS assumption, and a number of
mitigations for it have been attempted over the years: Pointcheval [37] pro-
vided a variant of the Okamoto-Schnorr blind signature scheme [35] that boosts
security using cut-and-choose to catch cheating behavior. Roughly speaking,
in PointchevalтАЩs ямБx, the user commits to two challenges at the beginning of

user has sent the challenge. However, the assumption requires larger parameters
for concrete security, discouraging its use in practice. Fuchsbauer and Wolf [24]
proposed a diямАerent approach, based on generic zero-knowledge proofs. Roughly
speaking, the user proves that the challenge has been generated correctly while
keeping private the blinding factors. This requires embedding the hash function
inside the proof, which is expensive in practice and limits the provable security
(since the hash function may not be formally modelled as a random oracle).
In our generic framework, we instead use Tessaro and ZhuтАЩs approach for
mitigating the ROS attack [43]. Roughly speaking, here the issuer commits to
a random value a when sending the commitment message of the ╬г-protocol,
and after receiving the challenge e from the user, sends a response that is valid
under challenge ea. This eямАectively re-randomizes the challenges and thwarts
ROS attacks making them statistically negligible [43]. We opt for this approach
because, despite the fact that the ямБnal transcript is slightly diямАerent from the
one of a ╬г-protocol, it presents strong security guarantees while only requiring
small changes to the initial ╬г-protocol that are easily adoptable by protocol
designers.

Oblivious VeriямБable Unpredictable Functions
We rely on oblivious zero-knowledge proofs to build an Oblivious VeriямБable
Unpredictable Function (OVUF) from pairing-free groups of prime order. As
in a veriямБable unpredictable function (VUF), a weakening of a VRF [33], we
consider a setting where an issuer holds a secret key sk, the user knows a public
key pk, and they engage in an interactive protocol to jointly evaluate a func-
tion Z = F(sk, m) of an input m chosen by the user. The user learns Z, along
with a proof ╧А that attests that Z = F(sk, m), which is veriямБed with help of the
public key. Crucially, however, we require this evaluation to be obliviousтАУthe
issuer does not learn anything about m, Z, and ╧А during the execution. We note
that this notion is stronger than that of a (veriямБable) OPRF, in that the latter
only provides veriямБability to the user, as the issuer provides a linkable proof of
evaluation which cannot be made public.
Before we turn to the formal treatment of OVUFs, and our construction,
we observe that an OVUF directly yields a blind signature scheme producing
signatures ╧Г = (F(sk, m), ╧А) for a message mтАУtherefore, the ямБrst part of the
signature is unique in that it only depends on m and sk. This is a natural
weakening of the notion of unique signatures [26], which suямГces in many of their
applications. To the best of our knowledge, no unique signatures, or partially
unique ones, are known in the pairing-free setting, let alone blind ones. This
is contrast to the pairings setting, where BLS signatures [7] and their blind
version [6] are unique. We expand on this further below.

Partially Unique Blind Signatures. We can think of the above OVUF protocol
as a blind signature scheme, with signatures of form ╧Г = (sk ┬╖ H(m), ╧А), where ╧А

veriямБed with the public key pk = sk ┬╖ G. We note that the signatures issued are

one-more unforgeability of the blind signature. Further, soundness guarantees
that for any valid signature we indeed have Z = sk ┬╖ H1(m), i.e., the Z part is
a deterministic function of m and sk. Such signatures (as in unique signatures)
can save veriямБcation costs by avoiding verifying multiple signatures for the same
message over and over. Once ╧Г = (sk ┬╖ H1(m), ╧А) is veriямБed, every following
signature on m will also contain the same sk ┬╖ H1(m) portion.
From a theoretical perspective, the resulting scheme oямАers an alternative to
existing blind signature schemes (e.g. [1,23,29,43]) in pairing-free groups. While
we rely on four messages, as opposed to three in these schemes, this diямАerence
is immaterial as three-message schemes also require two round trips.

approaches to anonymous tokens, as e.g. in PrivacyPass [11,17]. The origi-

Keyed-veriямБcation anonymous credentials (KVACs) [13] are typically con-
structed with an algebraic MAC, (this is a direct analog of anonymous credentials
constructed from blind signatures). In this approach, the issuer creates a MAC

verify ╧Г (unlike the blind signature case), the issuer also provides an issuance
proof ╧А that proves ╧Г is well-formed with respect to a commitment to the MAC
key and m. This is important for unlinkability of the credential, since otherwise
╧Г may be invalid in a way that is unique to the issuance session.
Here we apply our transform to blind ╧А, eямАectively making it a blind signa-
ture on m. This is interesting for three reasons. First, since ╧А can be veriямБed by
anyone, we can upgrade a KVAC to have some of the public veriямБability present
in traditional anonymous credential systems, while still retaining the very eямГ-
cient KVAC protocols for cases when public veriямБability is not required. Second,
there are many KVAC constructions (based on diямАerent algebraic MACs) we can
potentially upgrade in this way, with diямАerent features, tradeoямАs and assump-
tions. Third, this construction provides some of the functionality of anonymous
credentials with concurrent security.

A Provably Secure Variant of U-Prove
U-Prove is a set of cryptographic protocols initially designed by Stefan Brands
that allows users to minimally disclose information about what attributes are
encoded in a token. Each token is unlinkable, preventing tracking of users. We
focus on the issuance of a U-Prove token, which is illustrated in [36, Figure 8].
We provide a mitigation for the attack from Benhamouda et al. [5] for the case
where U-Prove tokens are generated using identical common inputs and the
computation is shared among parallel protocol executions. The resulting protocol
is almost identical to the previous one, except for the additional elements in the
proof transcript arising from the Tessaro-Zhu transform. The tokens themselves
