Abstract. We study the local leakage resilience of Shamir‚Äôs secret shar-
ing scheme. In Shamir‚Äôs scheme, a random polynomial f of degree t is
sampled over a Ô¨Āeld of size p > n, conditioned on f(0) = s for a secret s.
Any t shares (i, f(i)) can be used to fully recover f and thereby f(0). But,
any t‚ąí1 evaluations of f at non-zero coordinates are completely indepen-
dent of f(0). Recent works ask whether the secret remains hidden even
if say only 1 bit of information is leaked from each share, independently.
This question is well motivated due to the wide range of applications
of Shamir‚Äôs scheme. For instance, it is known that if Shamir‚Äôs scheme is
leakage resilient in some range of parameters, then known secure com-
putation protocols are secure in a local leakage model.
Over characteristic-2 Ô¨Āelds, the answer is known to be negative (e.g.,
Guruswami and Wootters, STOC ‚Äô16). Benhamouda, Degwekar, Ishai,
and Rabin (CRYPTO ‚Äô18) were the Ô¨Ārst to give a positive answer assum-
ing computation is done over prime-order Ô¨Āelds. They showed that if
t ‚Č•0.907n, then Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage resilient. Since then, there
has been extensive eÔ¨Äorts to improve the above threshold and after a
series of works, the current record shows leakage resilience for t ‚Č•0.78n
(Maji et al., ISIT ‚Äô22). All existing analyses of Shamir‚Äôs leakage resilience
for general leakage functions follow a single framework for which there is
a known barrier for any t ‚Č§0.5n.
In this work, we a develop a new analytical framework that allows us
to signiÔ¨Ācantly improve upon the previous record and obtain additional
new results. SpeciÔ¨Ācally, we show:
1. Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage resilient for any t ‚Č•0.69n.
2. If the leakage functions are guaranteed to be ‚Äúbalanced‚ÄĚ (i.e., split-
ting the domain of possible shares into 2 roughly equal-size parts),
then Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage resilient for any t ‚Č•0.58n.
3. If the leakage functions are guaranteed to be ‚Äúunbalanced‚ÄĚ (i.e.,
splitting the domain of possible shares into 2 parts of very diÔ¨Äerent
sizes), then Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage resilient as long as t ‚Č•0.01n.
Such a result is provably impossible to obtain using the previously
known technique.
All of the above apply more generally to any MDS codes-based secret
sharing scheme.
ConÔ¨Ārming leakage resilience is most important in the range t ‚Č§n/2,
as in many applications, Shamir‚Äôs scheme is used with thresholds t ‚Č§n/2.

Introduction
Secret sharing schemes, introduced by Shamir [35] and Blakley [6] are methods
that enable a dealer, that holds a secret piece of information, to distribute this
secret among n parties such that any subset of at least t parties can reconstruct
the secret, while subsets that contain fewer than t parties learn nothing about
it.
Secret sharing schemes are extremely useful in various applications across
multiple sub-areas of computer science, including cryptography, complexity, and
distributed computing and storage. Secret sharing schemes are also strongly
related to error correcting codes. We name just a few concrete applications where
secret sharing schemes are a fundamental building block: secure multiparty com-
putation protocols [3,11,17], threshold cryptography schemes [13,16,34], and
leakage-resilient circuit compilers [15,21,33].
At their basic use-case, secret sharing schemes have an ‚Äúall-or-nothing‚ÄĚ-style
security guarantee, wherein an adversary is allowed to corrupt up to t parties
but must know absolutely nothing about the others parties‚Äô shares. While this
assumption is often made in an ideal world, it is a very strong assumption in
many practical scenarios. Indeed, it has been long known that side-channel infor-
mation is widely available [22,23].
Research then has focused on designing new leakage resilient cryptographic
protocols in various models of leakage, for example, [2,7,14,30,31]. However, it is
also worth understanding whether existing schemes are leakage resilient. Indeed,
if an existing method is already leakage resilient, it alleviates the necessity to
design, analyse, and deploy new schemes. In this work, we focus on the latter
and continue the recent line of works studying the leakage resilience of Shamir‚Äôs
secret sharing scheme [1,5,25‚Äď28], the most well-known and useful secret sharing
scheme.
(Local) Leakage Resilience of Shamir‚Äôs Scheme. Shamir‚Äôs scheme is very
simple to describe: For a secret s, the dealer samples a random degree t ‚ąí1
(univariate) polynomial f over a suÔ¨Éciently large Ô¨Ānite Ô¨Āeld F, conditioned on
f(0) = s. Then, the dealer gives party i the Ô¨Āeld element f(i). Evaluations of f
at t diÔ¨Äerent points can be used to fully recover f and thereby s = f(0). Also, it is
known that any < t evaluations of f (excluding f(0)) are completely uncorrelated
with f(0). This scheme is the most commonly used (threshold) secret sharing
scheme, both in theory and in practice. This is mostly due to its simplicity,
elegance, and various useful features it supports like additive homomorphism
(i.e., linearity).

We consider local leakage attacks: In addition to fully learning some of the
shares, the attacker can leak few bits of information about each other parties‚Äô
share locally, independently of the other parties‚Äô state. That is, the adversary
can specify t‚Ä≤ < t indices i1, . . . , it‚Ä≤ ‚ąą[n] and n functions f1, . . . , fn with a short
output. Denoting the shares by ŌÄ1, . . . , ŌÄn, it then receives back ŌÄi1 . . . , ŌÄit‚Ä≤ and
f1(ŌÄ1), . . . , fn(ŌÄn), and it needs to guess (something about) f(0).
The motivation of the problem comes from that if Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage
resilient, then some applications that use it are more secure than what they
are currently known to be. For instance, the work of Benhamouda, Degwekar,
Ishai, and Rabin [4,5] (who initiated the study of the problem that we consider)
showed that Shamir‚Äôs scheme leakage resilience implies that a minor variant
of the Goldreich-Micali-Wigderson‚Äôs [17] secure computation protocol is secure
even in a local leakage model. Apart from its applications, understanding the
leakage resilience of Shamir‚Äôs scheme is a very natural question on its own right,
especially due to its connection to error correcting codes, and more precisely to
Reed-Solomon codes. (Shamir‚Äôs scheme is in particular a Reed-Solomon code.)
State of the Art. The local leakage resilience of Shamir‚Äôs scheme is far from
being understood. Despite signiÔ¨Ācant eÔ¨Äorts, there is essentially only one method
for analyzing it and for this method there are known barriers showing that it
cannot lead to full resolution of the problem. This method, based on Fourier
analysis over prime-order Ô¨Āelds, was introduced by [4] and since then all works
improving upon their bound, somewhat incrementally optimize various parts of
the analysis.
For concreteness and simplicity of presentation, we focus on the most studied
case where each leakage function (i.e., each fi) outputs a single bit. Benhamouda
et al.‚Äôs [4] original work showed that Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage resilient as long
as t ‚Č•0.907n. Then, Maji et al. [28] and Benhamouda et al. [5] independently
lowered this threshold to t ‚Č•0.8675n and t ‚Č•0.85n, respectively. The state of
the art is due to Maji et al. [27] who showed that Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage
resilient as long as t ‚Č•0.78n.
All of the above works, use an analytic proxy (introduced already in [4]‚Äôs
original work) for upper bounding the statistical distance between leakage dis-
tributions of diÔ¨Äerent secrets. Maji et al. [28] showed an inherent barrier for this
proof strategy: It is impossible to prove any meaningful result for any t ‚Č§0.5n
(which is required to, say, execute secure computation protocols in the honest
majority setting). It is worth noting that Shamir‚Äôs scheme is not leakage resilient
if t = O(n/ log n) [28,32].1
At a very high level, the analytical proxy bounds the statistical distance
between certain distributions via direct expansion and summing up the distances
at every point in the sample space. Then, a triangle inequality is performed
so that each term in the summation could be analyzed and bound by itself.

1.1
Our Results
In this work, we introduce a completely new analytical framework that, in par-
ticular, bypasses the barrier of the only previously known approach. We use our
approach to prove several new results that were out of reach previously. At a
very high level, we obtain our improvements because our analysis is roughly an
‚Ąď2 bound on the distance between the corresponding distributions, whereas the
previous approach is an ‚Ąď1 bound. In particular, we never apply lossy triangle
inequalities.
Using our new framework, we prove several new results for the leakage
resilience of Shamir‚Äôs scheme. In what follows, we assume that the leakage func-
tions output a single bit, either ‚ąí1 or 1. Further, we assume that the Ô¨Āeld size,
p, is at most 2O(n).2
1. We improve the previously best bound on the leakage resilience of Shamir‚Äôs
scheme from 0.78n to 0.69n. That is, Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage resilient as
long as t ‚Č•0.69n.
2. We further improve the threshold to 0.58n for all ‚Äúbalanced‚ÄĚ leakage func-
tions. That is, assuming that the leakage functions satisfy | E[fi]| ‚Č§c,3 where
c > 0 a universal constant, then Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage resilient as long
as t ‚Č•0.58n.
3. For the complementary case, where the leakage functions are unbalanced, we
break the t ‚Č•0.5n barrier. SpeciÔ¨Ācally, we show that if the leakage functions
satisfy | E[fi]| ‚Č•C with a speciÔ¨Āc constant C < 1, then Shamir‚Äôs scheme is
leakage resilient as long as t ‚Č•0.01n. We show that it is provably impossible
to obtain such a result using the previously known technique.
All of the results above directly generalize to the setting where the adversary
further obtains some of the shares in their entirety.4 Also, we note that all of
the above results apply to any MDS code-based secret sharing scheme (i.e., so
called Massey secret sharing schemes [29]) of which Shamir‚Äôs scheme is a special
case obtained by using the Reed-Solomon code (see Sect. 2.1).

of inputs fi outputs ‚ąí1. Note that intuitively, the balanced case has the highest
leakage of information.

1.2
Related Work
While the notion of local leakage may seem rather weak, it can be quite powerful.
In particular, it is not hard to see that if we work over a Ô¨Āeld F2k of characteristic
2, then a bit of the secret can be learnt by leaking just one bit from each share.
(This is true for any linear secret sharing scheme.) Surprisingly, for Shamir‚Äôs
scheme and in some settings of parameters, Guruswami and Wooters [20] showed
that full recovery of a multi-bit secret is possible by leaking only one bit from
each share.
Benhamouda et al. [4] were the Ô¨Ārst to study the local leakage resilience of
Shamir‚Äôs scheme (over prime-order Ô¨Āelds) showing that whenever t ‚Č•0.907n,
Shamir‚Äôs scheme is leakage resilient. Maji et al. [28] and Benhamouda et al. [5]
independently lowered the threshold to t ‚Č•0.8675n and t ‚Č•0.85n, respectively.
Before the current work, the record was leakage resilience whenever t ‚Č•0.78n
due to Maji et al. [27]. Nielsen and Simkin [32] presented an attack that requires
m > t log p/(n ‚ąít) bits of leakage from each secret share, where p is the Ô¨Āeld
size, and then guesses the secret with probability 1/2.
With the lack of progress in analyzing Shamir‚Äôs scheme for general leakage
functions, eÔ¨Äorts have been made to analyze restricted classes of leakage func-
tions or ‚Äúrandom‚ÄĚ constructions. Maji et al. [25] considered a leakage family
that is only allowed to leak an arbitrary single bit from each share (given in
their binary representation). This was later generalized to arbitrary bounded-
size families of leakage functions by Maji et al. [26]. Maji et al. [28] also proved
that a random (linear) secret sharing scheme is leakage resilient to one-bit local
leakage when t ‚Č•0.5n. This was partially derandomized in [26,28] who studied
the leakage resilience of Shamir‚Äôs scheme with random evaluation points.
All of the above works essentially use the analytic proxy from [4]‚Äôs work for
upper bounding the statistical distance between leakage distributions of diÔ¨Äerent
secrets. Maji et al. [28] showed that this technique cannot be used to go below
threshold t = n/2 for general leakage functions.

New (Non-linear) Schemes. A large body of work focused on designing new
leakage resilient secret sharing schemes from scratch. Such schemes were con-
structed for the Ô¨Ārst time by Dziembowski and Pietrzak [14]. Their scheme
involved an interactive reconstruction procedure, which was needed for allow-
ing the reconstruction to access only small part of the shares. Simpler construc-
tions (without the latter eÔ¨Éciency feature) were proposed by Dav`ńĪ et al. [12]. In
particular, they presented a simple two-party scheme based on any two-source
extractor, such as the inner-product extractor. More general constructions of
leakage-resilient secret-sharing schemes were given by Goyal and Kumar [18,19],
Srinivasan and Vasudevan [36], Kumar, Meka, and Sahai [24], Chattopadhyay et
al. [10], and Chandran et al. [8,9]. All of these works, design specialized secret
sharing schemes that have strong leakage resilience properties and/or apply to
more general access structures. It is noteworthy that in all of these works, the
schemes are non-linear, making them less applicable.

6.1
A Barrier of Previous Methods
It is already known that previous methods cannot prove local leakage resilience
of Shamir‚Äôs scheme for any t ‚Č§n/2 for general leakage functions. Indeed, [28]
showed a particular leakage function for which a proxy quantity in their analysis
becomes too large. The leakage function is the quadratic-residue function. This
leakage function is balanced and therefore cannot be used to claim that previous
techniques cannot be used to derive a result similar to what we get in Example 3.
We show an unbalanced variant of the quadratic-residue function for which a
similar barrier for previous techniques can be shown. We refer to Appendix B
for details.
