Abstract. We introduce and explicitly construct a quantum error-
detection code we coin a ‚ÄúPauli Manipulation Detection‚ÄĚ code (or PMD),
which detects every Pauli error with high probability. We apply them to
construct the Ô¨Ārst near-optimal codes for two tasks in quantum commu-
nication over adversarial channels.
Our main application is an approximate quantum code over qubits
which can eÔ¨Éciently correct from a number of (worst-case) erasure errors
approaching the quantum Singleton bound. Our construction is based
on the composition of a PMD code with a stabilizer code which is list-
decodable from erasures, a variant of the stabilizer list-decodable codes
studied by Leung and Smith [49], and Bergamaschi et al. [17].

gives an example of a task in quantum communication which is provably
impossible classically. Our construction is based on a combination of
PMD codes, stabilizer codes, and classical non-malleable codes (Dziem-

Approximate Quantum Erasure Codes on the Quantum Singleton
Bound. Our Ô¨Ārst application of PMD codes is to approximate quantum error
correction.

A challenging consequence of the relaxation to approximate erasure correc-

Our code construction is based on a recent approach to design quantum

Additional Related Work
Tamper-Detection Codes and Secret Sharing. Our work is inspired by
constructions of classical secret sharing schemes from ideas in tamper-resilient
cryptography [24‚Äď26,28,50]. A key ingredient in that line of work is the con-
cept of an algebraic manipulation detection (AMD) code [26]. Simply put, an
‚Äúalgebraic manipulation‚ÄĚ is a form of adversarial data tampering, without prior
knowledge of the data values. For instance, if our data is a value x in a group
G, then an ‚Äúalgebraic manipulation‚ÄĚ corresponds to adding some value őī ‚ąąG
(without knowing x), resulting in x+őī. An AMD code corresponds to an encod-
ing of data into G, in a way that any algebraic manipulation őī Őł= 0 is detected
with high probability. [44] generalized AMD codes to other tampering families,
including low-degree polynomial and low-depth circuit tampering.
[20] were the Ô¨Ārst to explore tamper-detection codes in the quantum setting.
Their work focused on the concept of a ‚ÄúUnitary Tamper Detection Code‚ÄĚ, a
quantum code which can faithfully detect errors chosen from a restricted family
of unitary operators. Among other results, they constructed explicit quantum
codes for classical messages which could detect arbitrary Pauli errors, which they
referred to as ‚ÄúQuantum AMDs‚ÄĚ. Our PMDs arose as a natural generalization
to quantum messages, albeit our techniques depart signiÔ¨Ācantly. Moreover, [20]
don‚Äôt attempt to address tampering channels (just unitaries), whose lack of
reversibility imply a unique set of challenges.
Approximate Quantum Error Correction. [48] were the Ô¨Ārst to showcase
the beneÔ¨Āts of approximate error correction to quantum communication. They
constructed a 4 qubit code which could correct a single amplitude dampening
error, violating the ‚ÄúHamming bound‚ÄĚ for quantum codes based on the Pauli
basis. Several results then studied conditions in which approximate quantum
error correction is even possible [12,14,15,30,41,47,51,52,55].
In this context, most related to our work are the results of [17,29,49], who
studied the adversarial error channel. While it is well known consequence of
the Knill-LaÔ¨āamme conditions [46] that no exact quantum code can correct
from more than n/4 adversarial errors, [29] constructed approximate quantum
codes which could correct (n ‚ąí1)/2 adversarial errors, all the way up to the
no-cloning bound! Their codes relied on an insightful combination of quantum
error correction, quantum authentication, and classical secret sharing, but came
at a cost of an exponentially large alphabet size and assymptotically decaying
rate.
[17,49] address these issues using the list-decoding of stabilizer codes. [49] pre-
sented a quantum code over qubits which beat the quantum Gilbert-Varshamov

shared between sender and receiver. [17] showed how to eÔ¨Éciently approach the
