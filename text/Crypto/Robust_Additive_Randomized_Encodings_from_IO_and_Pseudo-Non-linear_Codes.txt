Abstract. Additive randomized encodings (ARE), introduced by Halevi, Ishai,
Kushilevitz, and Rabin (CRYPTO 2023), reduce the computation of a k-party
function f(x1, . . . , xk) to locally computing encodings ˆxi of each input xi and
then adding them together over some Abelian group into an output encoding
ˆy =  ˆxi, which reveals nothing but the result. In robust ARE (RARE) the
sum of any subset of ˆxi, reveals only the residual function obtained by restrict-
ing the corresponding inputs. The appeal of (R)ARE comes from the simplicity
of the interactive part of the computation, involving only addition, which yields
for instance non-interactive multi-party computation in the shufﬂe model where
messages from different parties are anonymously shufﬂed. Halevi, Ishai, Kushile-
vitz, and Rabin constructed ARE from standard assumptions and RARE in the
ideal obfuscation model, leaving open the question of whether RARE can be
constructed in the plain model.
We construct RARE in the plain model from indistinguishability obfuscation,
which is necessary, and a new primitive that we call pseudo-non-linear codes. We
provide two constructions of this primitive assuming either Learning with Errors
or Decision Difﬁe Hellman. A bonus feature of our construction is that it is suc-
cinct. Speciﬁcally, encodings ˆxi can be decomposed to non-interactive parts ˆzi,
generated in time proportional to the input size, and sent directly to the evaluator,
and group parts ˆgi that are added together, and whose size depends only on the
security parameter.

phy. In the context of secure multi-party computation (MPC) [8,13,15,29], different

terms of efﬁciency and communication complexity. Recently, Halevi, Ishai, Kushilevitz,
and Rabin [19] introduced the notion of additive randomized encodings (ARE). Such
randomized encodings can be seen as a version of multi-party randomized encodings
[2] where securely computing a multi-party function is reduced to locally computing

Replacing Ideal Obfuscation with IO. Recall that in contrast to ideal obfuscation where
˜D behaves as a black box oracle, in the case of IO all that we are guaranteed is that

1.2
Open Questions
In our construction, the public parameters are highly structured (they consist of an
obfuscated program). Thus, an open question is constructing RARE with no setup, or
with a less structured setup, such as a common random string. In terms of the underlying
hardness assumptions, our construction relies on IO and PLNC. While IO is necessary,
we do not know whether that is also the case for PNLC, and in particular whether PNLC
can be constructed from IO. Another related open question is constructing RARE from
assumptions weaker than (or different from) IO by restricting attention to simpler func-
tions (rather than all efﬁcient ones).
We note that there are several more open questions concerning the more general
notion of (non-robust) ARE. We refer the reader to the open questions section in [19].
